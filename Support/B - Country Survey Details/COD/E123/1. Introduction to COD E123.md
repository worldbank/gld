# Introduction to Democratic Republic of the Congo Enquete 1-2-3 (COD E123)

## What is the COD E123?

The **Enquete 1-2-3 (E123)** is a multi-phase household survey program implemented in the **Democratic Republic of the Congo (COD)**. The name refers to its three linked phases: **Phase 1** covers employment and basic household characteristics, **Phase 2** covers the informal sector, and **Phase 3** covers household consumption. The harmonized rounds currently covered in the GLD repository use only **Phase 1**, because that is the phase containing the labor-market information needed for the GLD. In practical terms, the COD E123 rounds used in the GLD are therefore closer to employment-focused household surveys than to full multi-topic household budget surveys.

## What does the COD E123 cover?

The COD E123 harmonized rounds contain information on demographic characteristics, education, and labor market activities over the past 7 days. The harmonized years and sample sizes currently used in the GLD harmonization are as follows:

| Year | HH count | Individual count |
|---|---|---|
| 2004 | 13,215 | 72,685 |
| 2012 | 21,454 | 111,679 |

## Where can the data be found?

The datasets are not accessible to the public and researchers have to request the data from the Institut National de la Statistique (INS) of the Democratic Republic of the Congo. The World Bank has been granted access to the datasets, if you work or are part of the World Bank Group, kindly contact the Jobs Group with a formal request for access to gld@worldbank.org

## What is the sampling procedure?

The COD E123 rounds currently harmonized in the GLD do not share exactly the same sampling design, so users should keep the round-specific frame in mind when comparing the series.

For the 2004 round, the survey was not fielded as one single national operation from the start. The first rollout was conducted in **Kinshasa** in 2004. There, the neighborhood frame was stratified into 12 groups using living standard and neighborhood size, 50 quartiers were selected with probability proportional to population, and households were then listed and sampled within the selected quartiers. The consumption phase used a smaller sub-sample of those households. The broader provinces rollout followed in 2005. Outside Kinshasa, the design was stratified and multistage, with separate treatment of statutory cities, cités, and rural districts, followed by household selection within sampled quartiers or villages. This means that the 2004-2005 file combines a Kinshasa-first rollout with a later provinces survey rather than one fully simultaneous national design.

For the 2012 round, the report describes a national stratified multistage design. In practical terms, the survey was built to produce usable estimates at the national level, by province, and by urban-rural residence. Within each province, urban statutory cities and cités were sampled separately from rural territories, and households were selected from sampled neighborhoods, sectors, or villages. Kinshasa was treated separately and oversampled relative to the standard provincial allocation. Although the report discusses the newer provincial structure, the delivered microdata used in the GLD harmonization still identify the older 11-province scheme.

Because the delivered harmonized rounds contain the sampling weights, province identifiers, and urban-rural residence variables, the GLD uses those design variables directly. For applied work, the safe reading is that the data are designed for national estimates, province-level comparisons, and urban-rural breakdowns. Users should avoid interpreting districts, quartiers, or villages as equally reliable analytic domains unless the survey documentation says otherwise.

## What is the significance level?

The harmonized datasets contain the delivered sampling weights and province identifiers, so the series can be used for national estimates and province-level comparisons, with an additional urban-rural split. For the 2004-2005 materials, Kinshasa is often presented as its own domain, while the broader rollout is discussed by province and by urban-rural residence. For the 2012 report, many tables are shown for Kinshasa, other urban areas, urban, rural, and the national total, while other results are shown by province. In practical terms, this is the level at which the survey is meant to be read: national, province, and residence-area analysis, with Kinshasa often singled out within the urban domain.

## Other noteworthy aspects

### Fieldwork timing

The interview timing is uneven, especially in the 2012 round. This matters for data users because labor indicators in a survey with strong agricultural and informal-sector seasonality can reflect the timing of fieldwork as much as underlying annual labor-market conditions. Using the survey's collection-date variables, the 2012 fieldwork is concentrated late in the calendar year: most interviews took place in September, October, and November 2012, with a smaller spillover into early 2013. This implies that the 2012 round should not be read as a smoothly distributed month-by-month sample across the year.

The 2012 interview-year distribution is:

| Interview year | Observations | Share of sample |
|---|---:|---:|
| 2012 | 104,311 | 93.40% |
| 2013 | 7,368 | 6.60% |

The 2012 interview month-year distribution is:

| Interview month-year | Observations | Share of sample |
|---|---:|---:|
| 2012-09 | 8,595 | 7.70% |
| 2012-10 | 74,645 | 66.84% |
| 2012-11 | 20,963 | 18.77% |
| 2012-12 | 96 | 0.09% |
| 2013-01 | 79 | 0.07% |
| 2013-02 | 4,884 | 4.37% |
| 2013-03 | 2,400 | 2.15% |
| 2013-04 | 5 | 0.00% |
| Year-month not fully identified | 12 | 0.01% |

For this reason, users should be especially cautious about treating the 2012 round as a cleanly year-round cross-section when comparing it with other rounds. The 2004 harmonized file does not contain an interview-month variable, so the same check cannot be carried out there from the delivered microdata.

### Employment: Industry classification

The two harmonized E123 rounds are not fully comparable in industry coding. The 2004 round uses an industry coding structure that is closest to **ISIC Rev. 3.1**, while the 2012 round uses auxiliary activity files and secondary-job recodes aligned more closely with **ISIC Rev. 4**. The GLD codes each round conservatively within its own classification framework. This implies that cross-year comparisons using detailed industry codes should be made with caution.

### Employment: Occupation classification

Occupation information is also less comparable across the available rounds. In the 2004 round, the delivered occupation codes are close to a structured occupation classification and can be harmonized conservatively. In the 2012 round, the delivered main and secondary job occupation variables are grouped 3-digit codes that align with ISCO-88, but the round still differs from 2004 in coding structure and reference materials. For this reason, users should not assume that harmonized occupation variables are fully comparable across the two rounds even though both rounds now carry structured occupation coding in the GLD.

### Wages

The wage variables rely on the information available in each round and should be interpreted with care. In particular, the 2012 round includes both exact monthly earnings and bracketed earnings responses. The GLD codes exact values where available and converts bracketed responses into numeric values following the standard GLD wage guidance. This makes the wage variables usable, but it also means that some 2012 earnings are interval-based approximations rather than exact reported amounts.

### Questionnaire and raw-data differences

There are cases in which the raw data are less detailed than the questionnaire would suggest. The GLD harmonization only codes variables that are clearly supported by the delivered data. For this reason, some variables that appear possible from the questionnaire remain missing in the harmonized output when the corresponding raw detail is not sufficiently reliable or complete.
