## What is the GHA LFS?

The Ghana Labour Force Survey (LFS) is a household survey conducted by the Ghana Statistical Service (GSS) to collect information on the labour force situation and labour market conditions in Ghana. The survey provides statistics on employment, unemployment, underemployment, labour force participation, hours worked, earnings, and related labour market characteristics.

The GLD harmonization currently includes the 2015 Ghana LFS. According to the official report, the 2015 LFS was the first stand-alone labour force survey conducted in Ghana; before that, labour force information was mainly collected through the Ghana Living Standards Survey (GLSS), including the GLSS6 labour force module.

## What does the GHA LFS cover?

The Ghana LFS collects information on household and individual demographic characteristics, education, current and usual economic activity, employment, unemployment, underemployment, employment status, employment sector, occupation, industry, hours worked, wages and earnings, second jobs, informal employment, youth employment and school-to-work transition, migration, agricultural activities, non-farm enterprises, occupational injuries and safety, industrial disputes, non-market economic activity, and selected housing conditions.

## Where can the data be found?

The official 2015 Labour Force Report is publicly available through the Ghana Statistical Service (GSS) publication website. The ILO Survey Catalogue also provides metadata for the Ghana Labour Force Survey 2015, including information on the producer, scope, coverage, and related classifications.

The microdata used for GLD harmonization should be treated as part of the internal GLD/Jobs Group holdings unless a public-use version is confirmed through the Ghana Statistical Service microdata catalogue or another official data access channel. World Bank users who need access to the harmonized files should contact the Jobs Group.

## What is the sampling procedure?

The 2015 Ghana LFS used a two-stage stratified random sampling design. The sampling frame was based on the complete list of census Enumeration Areas (EAs) from the 2010 Population and Housing Census. These EAs were used as the primary sampling units (PSUs). Institutional populations, such as people in schools and hospitals, were excluded from the sampling frame.

In the first stage, 402 EAs were selected. The EAs were stratified by Ghana’s ten administrative regions and by urban/rural location, creating 20 strata. In the second stage, 15 households were selected from each sampled EA, producing an initial sample of 6,030 households. Of these, 5,838 households were successfully interviewed.

Before interviews were conducted, a listing exercise was carried out in each selected EA to identify households. Data collection was conducted using Computer Assisted Personal Interviewing (CAPI). Household information was collected from the household head or another knowledgeable adult household member, while labour force information for household members aged 15 years and older was collected directly from each eligible individual where possible.

The data were weighted to account for the probability of selecting the PSU and the probability of selecting the household within the PSU. Since the design was not self-weighting, sampling weights were required to account for differences in selection probabilities and completed interviews across PSUs.

## What is the geographic significance level?

The official report states that the 2015 LFS was designed to provide reliable and disaggregated labour market statistics at the national, regional, and zonal levels. The sample was stratified by region and urban/rural location, and many official tables report results by region, locality, and sex.

In practice, national-level estimates, urban/rural estimates, and regional estimates should generally be considered the main geographic levels for reporting. More detailed cross-tabulations, especially those combining region, sex, age group, industry, occupation, or employment status, should be used with caution because cell sizes may become small.

## Other noteworthy aspects

### First stand-alone LFS

The 2015 Ghana LFS is noteworthy because it was the first stand-alone labour force survey conducted in Ghana. Earlier labour force information was collected through the Ghana Living Standards Survey, especially the GLSS6 labour force module. For this reason, users should be careful when comparing the 2015 LFS with earlier Ghana labour indicators from GLSS rounds, as survey design, questionnaire structure, and labour modules may differ.

### ICLS-13 / ICLS-19 Employment Mapping

The survey is implemented following ICLS-19 standards, and the harmonized `icls_v` variable is set to 19 to indicate the baseline classification framework.

Farm work carried out mainly for sale or barter (`s3aq3e == 1`) qualifies as employment under both ICLS-13 and ICLS-19 definitions. Farm work mainly intended for own household consumption (`s3aq3e == 2`) is excluded from the employment count under ICLS-19, as it constitutes own-use production work rather than work performed for pay or profit.

For analyses requiring ICLS-13 comparability, respondents with `s3aq3e == 2` can be brought back into employment. Under this back-compatibility coding, subsistence farmers are assigned status in employment as self-employed, occupation code 63, and industry code 01. Users should note that the ICLS-13 mapping is approximate — not fully precise but considered acceptable for comparative purposes.
