# Introduction to Indonesian labor force survey – Survei Angkatan Kerja Nasional (SAKERNAS)


## What is the IDN SAKERNAS?

The National Labor Force Survey (or Survei Angkatan Kerja Nasional, SAKERNAS) is a household-based sample survey conducted by Badan Pusat Statistik (BPS) – Statistics Indonesia. Starting from 1976, SAKERNAS was implemented firstly as a module of the National Socio-Economic Survey (SUSENAS). It was separated from SUSENAS and conducted quarterly since 1986. 


## What does the IDN SAKERNAS cover?

The IDN SAKERNAS mainly covers demographic information, including age, sex, marital status, location, education, and labor market activities over the previous 7 days. In more recent years (i.e., 2016–2024), SAKERNAS has become more detailed and includes additional information on individuals’ health conditions and social security status. The years used for harmonization span 1989 to 2024, except for 1995, which was dropped because SAKERNAS was not conducted in that year. We did not harmonize 1995, although the 1995 Intercensal Population Survey could potentially be used as a substitute.

Since 2005, two rounds have been available for harmonization each year: the February round and the August round. We used the round with the larger sample size, which is usually the August round. The only exception is 2005, when the February round had a larger sample size. Over time, there has been a clear expansion in both the number of variables collected and the range of topics covered in each year’s survey.

## Where can the data be found?

The microdata of SAKERNAS are not publicly available. The raw data we used were acquired by filling in a data request form and then shared by the World Bank Indonesia Team. Data can also be purchased from the BPS' Statistics Service Information System ([link here](https://silastik.bps.go.id/v3/index.php/site/login/) - requires login)


## What is the sampling procedure?

SAKERNAS uses a two-stage cluster sampling method in general, which includes two steps: 1) selecting the main sampling unit – census blocks and 2) selecting the ultimate sampling unit – households within each main sampling unit. As the frequency of data collection and level of data representativeness have changed over time, the implementation of sample selection methods also changed. For example, year 2007 is a dividing line in that all years before 2007 can only produce representative estimates up to province level whereas all years after 2007 (including 2007) have representativeness at district level except for the August round of 2016. The exception of year 2016 is because of the reduction on its sample size, which was reduced from 200,000 households to 50,000 households. The knowledge product, A Guide to Working with Indonesian Survey Data (hereinafter referred to as The Guide), written by the Poverty Global Practice of the World Bank Group outlines the sampling procedure as:

Probability Proportional to Size method (PPS) is used when selecting census block from Master Sampling Frame (MSF) which contains the list of primary sampling units (PSU or wilcah/wilayah pencacahan). Sampling frame for the annual Sakernas was developed using the results of population census conducted previously before the respective Sakernas was conducted, while sampling frame for the quarterly Sakernas was obtained from the sampling frame of Susenas. For example, sampling frame for Sakernas 2003 was derived from the results of 2000 Population Census (SP2000), while sampling frame used in Sakernas 2011 was coming from Susenas 2011 sampling frame.

After census blocks were selected, a systematical random sampling method is then used to select households within census block (16 households per census block before 2011 and 10 households per census block since 2011). To describe more clearly about how the sampling is done in Sakernas, this chapter covers two specific examples, i.e. for Sakernas 2007 to 2010 and 2011 to 2015.

In Sakernas 2007 to 2010, the Master Sampling Frame was developed using the results of 2006 Economic Census. Sampling procedure was done by district/city and was separated between urban and rural areas. For each district/city, there were two stages sampling method as follows:

First stage: From the MSF, n(h) census blocks were selected (h=1 for urban and h=0 for rural) using Probability Proportional to Size (PPS) method.
Second stage: From each census block that had been selected in the first stage, 16 households were selected using a systematic random sampling method.

In Sakernas 2011 to 2015, the Master Sampling Frame is developed using the results of 2010 Population Census (SP2010), which has been updated every time before the survey will take place. Starting from 2011, the period in which Sakernas began to be conducted quarterly, household samples are selected using three-stage withdrawal, with the following steps:

First stage: From the MSF, 30,000 PSUs are selected for Susenas sampling frame by using the method of PPS. These 30,000 PSUs are then randomly allocated evenly for each quarter of Susenas, i.e. 7,500 PSU for each quarter. From 7,500 PSUs of Susenas Quarter I, 5,000 PSUs were systematically selected to be used for Sakernas Quarter I (February), and will be used again for Sakernas Quarters II (May), III (August), and IV (November).
Second stage: From each PSU in the 5,000 selected PSUs, two census blocks were selected using PPS method, proportional to number of households based on SP2010 results. These selected census blocks were then randomly allocated for Susenas and Sakernas (1 census block for Susenas and 1 census block for Sakernas). The selected census blocks for Sakernas were utilized for provincial estimates and divided into 4 sample packages, each of which consists of 1,250 census blocks.
Third stage: From each census block selected in the second stage, 10 households were selected using systematic random sampling.

Since 2019, the sample size has been increased to 75,000 households in the February round and 300,000 households in the August round. In the recent period, including 2020–2024, SAKERNAS has continued to be conducted semi-annually, with the February round producing estimates up to the province level and the August round producing estimates up to the district/city level.
  
  
## What is the geographic significance level?

The official reports present results by gender, sector (urban or rural), province, and district (kabupaten/kota). Province is the first administrative level for which the data are representative for 1989–2006, while district is the second administrative level for which the data are representative for 2007–2024, except for the August 2016 round. In the harmonized dataset, the variable subnatid1_prev1 shows how provincial boundaries and identifiers change over time. More details on historical changes in administrative divisions can be found in the separate [document](Geographic_Significance_Level.md).


## Other noteworthy aspects


### Unique Household ID Generation

Household IDs are not directly available in SAKERNAS regardless of year. Location identifiers such as province, district, sector (urban or rural) etc. can be used to generate household IDs. However, these location identifiers are not necessarily fully available in all survey years. We created household IDs using STATA codes provided by the *The Guide* for years that have the full list of location identifiers required.

![STATA_HHID_codes](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/84488698/163726938-8dfafff8-f195-442f-bef7-6f3a9f9843f9.png)

Household IDs were only created if 1) the given year has the full list of identifiers and 2) the number of households generated using this method matches the household sample size in *The Guide*. Because *The Guide* only provides a range of household sample size for several years in rounded number, it is possible that our self-created household number is not equal to the exact number of household in a specific year. ### Definition of Labor Status and Labor Force Participation

From 1989 to 2024, the structure of SAKERNAS, especially the labor force module, has changed greatly. Not only has the number of labor-related questions increased, but the logic as well as the order of questions has also changed. Although our definition of the GLD variables lstatus and potential_lf never changes, we still needed to find the most appropriate question or set of questions in the survey to define the two variables, which sometimes required slight adaptation. To make sure that we understood the intention and logic of the questions and made adaptations correctly, we used labor force participation rates reported in the International Labour Organization (ILO) microdata library as a benchmark for years with adaptations; for example, the ILO microdata library provides a detailed report for the 2015 August round. However, only 2014–2017 appear to have detailed breakdowns of employed population, unemployed population, and non-labor force population available in that source. For the more recent period, including 2020–2024, we also used official BPS labor market publications based on SAKERNAS as an additional benchmark, since BPS continues to publish labor force indicators and labor force situation tables for the February and August rounds. We also used World Bank country indicators
 as a general reference. Here are some brief explanations. A more comprehensive explanation of the harmonization choices follows. [is covered here in a separate document](Labor_Status_and_Labor_Force_Participation.md).    

All the harmonized years can be grouped into three categories:

1989-1999: the questionnaires of these years follow the same structure and have the same labor force module questions. Simply following the order of the questions can locate all the conditions needed to decide the employed, the unemployed, and non-labor force population.
2000-2016: the labor force module has become more complete (and complex). Questions are more well-guided during this time period. The Only for Working Household Members block states clearly the requirements for people to answer this block, which is a clear clue to code employed people.
2017-2024: the questionnaires from 2017 onward follow the more up-to-date structure, which adds more details on issues such as work environment, welfare, and, in some recent years, other employment-related conditions. Yet, while the block on employment status questions increases and expands, the previous order was discarded and the guide becomes more blurred. Following the logic of the questionnaire would not always produce sound labor force participation rates. We therefore slightly changed the way we coded lstatus so as to get close to the ILO estimates. For the more recent years, including 2020-2024, we also checked the harmonized estimates against official BPS SAKERNAS labor market publications. In addition, starting from the February 2024 round, BPS states that the questionnaire refers to the ILO Labor Force Survey questionnaire module.

Finally, it is worth pointing out that the age required for the education and labor force modules changed between 2016 and 2017: it is 10 years old for 1989-2016, whereas it is 5 years old for 2017-2024.     


### Coding of industry and occupation codes

Regarding industrial classification, 5 versions of Indonesian Standard Industrial Classification (KBLI) were involved from 1989 to 2024 – KLUI1997, KBLI2000, KBLI2005, KBLI2009, and KBLI2015. They were based on different versions of ISIC and were used in different years of SAKERNAS. in the [separate document.](Correspondence_National_International_Classifications.md) 

Although four versions of Indonesian Standard Occupational Codes (KBJI) were involved – KBJI1982, KBJI2000, KBJI2002, KBJI2014, we only used KBJI1982 and KBJI2002 during our mappiong process. Some years of SAKERNAS provide three versions of KBJI in the raw datasets yet with differet degrees of specifications. For example, SAKERNAS 2011 has KBJI 1982 three digits, 2000 1 digit and 2002 1 digit. But KBJI1982 three digits is the version we used most since it carries more information on occupation. 

Details about industrial and occuaptional mappings are covered in this [separate document.](Correspondence_National_International_Classifications.md) 

### Occupation code: KBJI Harmonization Decisions for 2000 and 2016–2017

For 2000, the occupation code uses KBJI 2000, which differs from the coding system used in the surrounding years. Because this creates a break in comparability and we did not identify a reliable correspondence that would allow us to map it consistently with the rest of the series, we coded occupation as missing for that year. For 2016 and 2017, the raw data contain both 1-digit KBJI 2002/2014 variables and a 4-digit KBJI 2002 variable. Although the 4-digit KBJI 2002 code provides more detailed information, its classification structure differs from the one used in the surrounding years, which makes the resulting categories appear as outliers and reduces consistency over time. We therefore chose to use the 1-digit KBJI 2014 classification for 2016 and 2017 to maintain comparability across years. Anyone interested in using the more detailed alternative classification is welcome to retrieve the relevant KBJI 2002 code directly from the raw data. For reference, the concordance between KBJI 2014 and KBJI 2002 is available here: https://ppid.bps.go.id/upload/doc/Tabel_Kesesuaian_KBJI_2014_dengan_KBJI_2002_1659512133.pdf

### No education in 2023-2024

In 2023 and 2024, the survey question on educational attainment combines individuals with no formal education and those with incomplete primary education into a single category, making it impossible to distinguish between the two groups. Based on patterns observed in earlier years, the proportion of individuals with incomplete primary education is substantially higher than those with no education. To maintain consistency and avoid underrepresenting the larger group, we classified all individuals in this combined category as having incomplete primary education.([questionnaire](utilities/idn_educ_2023.png)
